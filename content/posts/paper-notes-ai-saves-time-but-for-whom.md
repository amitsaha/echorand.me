Some highlights that stood out for me from the paper, [AI saves time - but for whom? A qualitative study of clinicians, AI developers and competing notions of efficiency in healthcare](https://jmepb.bmj.com/content/2/2/e000104) by Aurelia Sauerbrei.

I bring my bias into this reading, of course. My bias is I am leaning towards more understanding (and slower doing) of the role of AI as we make it the hammer whom we 
want to hit every nail with. 


## Ideal goals or the pitch of AI solutions

> "Similarly, an independent evaluation of the NHS AI Lab emphasises the efficiency gains resulting from AI deployment within care pathways"

> "A systematic review and metaanalysis has shown that these ideal characteristics have a small but significant effect on
health outcomes"

## Role of technical interventions

> "While very limited research exists on AI systems specifically, research on health-service delivery and digital health technologies, such as electronic health records
(EHRs) and other efficiency-promoting technologies, such as telehealth services, suggests that there is no simple opposition between efficiency and relational aspects of
care." 

> "Reviews of electronic medical records and eHealth interventions report mixed effects on communication, trust and patient–provider relationships, while some
digital tools, such as patient portals, have been associated in some contexts with improved information-sharing, patient engagement and patient satisfaction."
>
> More recent conceptual work likewise suggests that understanding the effects of digital health technologies on the physician–patient relationship requires attending to the
relational and contextual conditions of care, not to the technology alone
